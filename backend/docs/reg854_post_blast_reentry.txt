O. Reg 854 s.119/286 - Post-blast re-entry and toxic gases (CO, NO2)

NOTE: Excerpted plain-language working copy for offline edge use. Not the
official consolidated regulation.

Blasting underground produces carbon monoxide (CO) and nitrogen dioxide (NO2)
along with other gases. These must be cleared by ventilation before workers
return to the face. Carbon monoxide is the governing gas for re-entry because it
is produced in large quantity and clears more slowly than the others, so a safe
CO reading is the practical signal that the air has recovered.

(1) After a blast, no worker shall return to the affected area until ventilation
has cleared the smoke and gases and the atmosphere has been tested and found
within safe limits. Re-entry is judged on measured gas concentrations, not on a
fixed waiting time alone.

(2) Carbon monoxide shall be controlled below the applicable occupational limit
(commonly taken as 25 ppm as an 8-hour average) before unprotected re-entry. A
short-term exposure limit governs brief exposures during the clearing period.

(3) Nitrogen dioxide is acutely toxic at low concentrations and shall be treated
as a serious hazard even when carbon monoxide appears to be clearing. It must be
ventilated out and re-tested; workers shall not rely on odour to judge its
presence.

Re-entry intent: ramping ventilation immediately after the blast shortens the
clearance time and lets the crew return to the face sooner and more safely. The
fixed monitor on the level provides the confirming readings for re-entry.
